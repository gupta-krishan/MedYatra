Medical Tourism in India — Complete Guide 2025

Why Choose India for Medical Treatment?

India is the world's fastest-growing medical tourism destination, welcoming over 1.4 million international patients annually. The primary reason is cost — medical treatment in India costs 60–80% less than in the USA, UK or Europe, with no compromise on quality. Hospitals like Apollo, Medanta and Tata Memorial hold JCI accreditation — the same gold standard as American hospitals. Surgeons are internationally trained, equipment is imported from the USA and Europe, and English is the working language at all major centres.

Medical Tourism Cost Comparison: India vs USA

Bypass surgery costs $4,500–$7,000 in India vs $80,000–$120,000 in the USA. Knee replacement costs $5,000–$8,500 in India vs $35,000–$70,000. IVF treatment costs $2,500–$5,500 per cycle in India vs $15,000–$30,000. Kidney transplant costs $12,000–$20,000 in India vs $150,000–$300,000. Liver transplant costs $25,000–$45,000 in India vs $300,000–$600,000.

Top Hospitals in India for International Patients

The best hospitals in India for medical tourism include Apollo Hospitals (Chennai, Delhi, Mumbai), Medanta The Medicity (Gurugram), Narayana Health (Bengaluru, Kolkata), Tata Memorial Centre (Mumbai), AIIMS Delhi, Fortis Memorial Research Institute (Gurugram), CMC Vellore, Escorts Heart Institute (Delhi) and NIMHANS (Bengaluru). All hold JCI or NABH international accreditation.

Medical Visa for India — How to Apply

International patients need an Indian Medical e-Visa (e-Medvisa), applied online at indianvisaonline.gov.in. Requirements include a hospital invitation letter, medical reports, passport and return flight details. Approval takes 3–5 business days. MedYatra prepares your invitation letter as part of the $40 Premium Plan. The visa allows a 60-day stay and can be extended for ongoing treatment.

Countries That Send the Most Patients to India

The top source countries for medical tourism to India are Bangladesh (360,000 patients/year), Afghanistan (180,000), Iraq (95,000), Nigeria (85,000), Kenya (70,000), South Africa (55,000), USA (48,000), Oman (42,000), United Kingdom (38,000) and Canada (30,000). Patients from the USA come to avoid high insurance costs. Patients from the UK come to avoid long NHS waiting lists. Patients from Africa and the Middle East come because many procedures are unavailable or too expensive in their home countries.
